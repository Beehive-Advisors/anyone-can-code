# Lecture 1.4 — Memory Hierarchy

**Section:** Computer Fundamentals
**Prerequisites:** 1.2 (Logic Gates and Circuits), 1.3 (The CPU: Fetch, Decode, Execute)
**Length target:** ~12 minutes
**Animations:** 9

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you'll be able to:

1. Name the five layers of the memory hierarchy in order and explain why each one exists.
2. Describe the speed-cost-capacity tradeoff that shapes every computer ever built.
3. Explain what temporal and spatial locality are, and why caching only works because programs exhibit both.
4. State, in cycles, roughly how much a cache miss costs on modern hardware — and why that number shocks most programmers.
5. Recognize the difference between latency and bandwidth, and avoid the "SSDs are basically RAM" trap.

## Opening Hook

**SPEAK**
> "Your laptop has, let's say, 16 gigabytes of RAM. Sixteen billion bytes. And RAM is fast — on the order of nanoseconds. A nanosecond is a billionth of a second. So here's the puzzle: if RAM is already that fast, why does your CPU have *three* levels of cache sitting between it and RAM? Why three? What is your processor hiding from?"

**SPEAK**
> "The answer is the central story of this lecture, and it's one of the most important pieces of intuition you can carry into the rest of the course. Every computer you've ever used is built around trying to outrun the speed of its own memory."

## Part 1 — Registers: where computation actually happens

### What is a register?

**SPEAK**
> "In the last lecture we built up the CPU — fetch, decode, execute — and we met something called a register. Let's anchor that idea before we go anywhere new. A register is a tiny storage slot wired directly into the CPU core, right next to the ALU. And here's the key rule: the ALU can only operate on values that are sitting in registers. Nothing else. Every time your program does arithmetic, the numbers involved are in registers, period."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![register-closeup](./animations/register-closeup.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/register-closeup.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "Notice how every value that touches the ALU came from a register and every result lands back in a register. Outside this tight loop, the CPU can't compute on anything. An x86 CPU has sixteen of these general-purpose registers. That's it. Sixteen tiny slots, maybe a hundred bytes of total state. Everything else in your program — every variable, every array, every database row — has to make its way *into* one of those slots before the CPU can do anything with it."

## Part 2 — The Memory Wall

### Why one layer isn't enough

**SPEAK**
> "So if registers are so fast, the obvious question is: why not make everything a register? Two reasons. First, registers have to sit physically next to the ALU, and there's only so much silicon real-estate there. Second, the faster you make a memory cell, the more transistors it takes — so registers are expensive per byte. And over the last twenty years, CPUs got roughly a hundred times faster while main memory barely moved. That gap, widening year after year, is called the memory wall. It's why the rest of this lecture even exists."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![hierarchy-pyramid](./animations/hierarchy-pyramid.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/hierarchy-pyramid.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "This is the shape the industry converged on. Five tiers, each slower and cheaper and bigger than the one above it. Each tier exists because the tier below it isn't fast enough, and the tier above it isn't big enough. Every layer is a compromise. And this pyramid is the map we'll follow for the rest of the lecture — keep it in your head."

## Part 3 — L1, L2, and L3 Cache

### Why there are three levels, not just one

**SPEAK**
> "Let's zoom into the top of the pyramid. Between your registers and your RAM, the CPU has up to three layers of cache — called L1, L2, and L3. The obvious question: why three? Couldn't we just have one big cache?"

**SPEAK**
> "We can't, because cache itself has the same tradeoff as everything else. A small cache is very fast but misses often. A big cache catches more hits but has to be physically larger, which makes it slower to search. The answer: build several, each at a different compromise point. L1 is tiny — maybe 32 kilobytes per core — and it runs in about 4 clock cycles. L2 is a few hundred kilobytes, around 10 cycles. L3 is measured in megabytes, shared across cores, around 30 cycles."

**SPEAK**
> "But the real reason caches are faster than RAM isn't size — it's physics. Caches use a different kind of memory cell entirely."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![sram-vs-dram-cell](./animations/sram-vs-dram-cell.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/sram-vs-dram-cell.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "SRAM — what caches are made of — uses six transistors per bit. It's stable; once you write a value it stays. DRAM — what main memory is made of — uses one transistor plus one tiny capacitor. The capacitor *holds* the bit as a trickle of electric charge, and that charge leaks away every few microseconds. Every single bit of your RAM has to be refreshed *thousands of times per second* just to keep holding its value. That refresh, plus the simpler circuit, is why DRAM is slower — and cheaper per byte — than SRAM. Caches and RAM aren't the same technology. They're fundamentally different hardware solving fundamentally different tradeoffs."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![cache-levels-nested](./animations/cache-levels-nested.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/cache-levels-nested.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "Watch the cycle counter at the top. An L1 hit: four cycles. An L1 miss that hits L2: ten. L2 miss into L3: thirty. Total miss that reaches RAM: about 350 cycles. Every miss is a step down the pyramid, and every step roughly triples the wait. The layers exist to make sure *most* of your memory accesses never have to take that full journey."

## Part 4 — Locality of Reference

### Why caching works at all

**SPEAK**
> "Everything we just described is a bet. The hardware is betting that once you touch an address, you'll touch it again soon. And that if you touch address X, you'll probably touch X+1 next. If that bet is wrong, caches are useless — they just add overhead. So why isn't it wrong? Because real programs follow two patterns so universal they have names. Temporal locality: you just touched something, you'll touch it again. Think of a loop counter, or a hot variable. Spatial locality: you just touched X, you'll touch its neighbors. Think of iterating through an array."

**SPEAK**
> "Caches are built to exploit both of these — and the way they exploit spatial locality is through something called a cache line."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![cache-line-64-bytes](./animations/cache-line-64-bytes.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/cache-line-64-bytes.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "Caches don't fetch one byte. They fetch an entire line — typically 64 bytes, enough for 8 of your program's 8-byte values — as one atomic unit. So when you read one integer from an array, the CPU quietly drags in the next fifteen for free. If your next access is a neighbor, it's already there. That's spatial locality being rewarded. If your next access is somewhere random, those 15 bytes you brought in go unused, and you pay for a whole new fetch. This is why *how* you walk through memory can matter more than *how much* math you do."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![sequential-vs-random](./animations/sequential-vs-random.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/sequential-vs-random.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "Same data, same count, same arithmetic. The only difference is the order. Sequential access rides the cache line: every fourth access or so triggers a fetch, the other three are free. Random access gets nothing: every access is probably a miss, every miss is a fresh 64-byte fetch, and 63 of those bytes are wasted. This is why the same algorithm can be ten times faster or slower depending on memory layout."

## Part 5 — Persistent Storage: SSDs and HDDs

### The floor of the hierarchy

**SPEAK**
> "Everything we've talked about so far — registers, caches, RAM — disappears when you turn the machine off. Those are all volatile. To keep anything across a reboot — your files, the operating system itself, the program you're running — you need persistent storage. That's the bottom two tiers of the pyramid. And the price of persistence is another massive slowdown."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![hdd-internals](./animations/hdd-internals.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/hdd-internals.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "A hard drive has physical moving parts. To read a random byte, the head arm has to swing to the right track — that's the seek — and then wait for the platter to spin around — that's rotational latency. Together, about 13 milliseconds. An NVMe SSD has no moving parts at all; it just reads from silicon. About 100 microseconds. The difference is roughly 130 to one — and that's before we even compare either of them to RAM."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![latency-log-ladder](./animations/latency-log-ladder.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/latency-log-ladder.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "Let's put the whole hierarchy on a human scale. If L1 cache is one second, RAM is almost two minutes. An SSD is a day and a night. An HDD is *four months*. These are the gaps the memory wall is hiding from you. And here's the misconception I want to kill: people will tell you modern SSDs are basically RAM. They are not. Bandwidth — the *rate* at which bytes flow — has gotten close. But latency — the time before the first byte arrives — is still a thousand times worse. Those two numbers behave very differently, and latency is what dominates random access."

## Part 6 — Why Cache Misses Matter More Than You Think

### The hidden cost

**SPEAK**
> "Let me close with the single most important number in this lecture. When you have a cache miss that reaches main memory, the CPU stalls for roughly 350 cycles. In those same 350 cycles, a modern core could have issued about a thousand other instructions. That's the hidden cost of a cache miss: not 'some nanoseconds' — a thousand instructions of silence."

**SHOW-ANIMATION**
[![the-stall](./animations/the-stall.gif)](https://videos.tail581af8.ts.net/1.4/the-stall.mp4)

**EXPLAIN**
> "A thousand instructions. For one miss. This is why people who care about performance spend so much time worrying about data layout and access patterns — because an algorithm with a pretty big-O can be beaten by an uglier algorithm that plays nice with the cache. You won't need to manage any of this by hand. Your compiler and your CPU's prefetcher do most of the work. But when a program is mysteriously slow, the answer is almost always: *it's waiting for memory*. Now you know what that means, and you know why."

## Putting It Together

**SPEAK**
> "Five tiers. Fast and small at the top, slow and vast at the bottom. The CPU can only operate on registers, so everything has to get there eventually — and the whole hierarchy exists to make that journey as short as possible, most of the time. Caches work because programs are predictable: you touch the same things repeatedly and you touch their neighbors. Every layer you added from L1 down to HDD is roughly a thousand times slower than the one above it. And a single trip from the top of the pyramid to the bottom costs you a thousand instructions you'll never get back."

**SPEAK**
> "When you read about a 'performance bottleneck' from now on, there's a good chance what it actually means is: some piece of data had to make that journey more times than anybody planned for. The hierarchy is the stage, and every program you write performs on it — whether you're paying attention or not."
