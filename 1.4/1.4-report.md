# Research Report: Lecture 1.4 — Memory Hierarchy

## Overview

Modern CPUs are bottlenecked not by how fast they compute but by how fast data can reach them. A 3.5 GHz core executes roughly 4 instructions per clock cycle, yet a single trip to main memory costs ~100 nanoseconds — about 350 cycles, or over 1,000 instructions' worth of stalled potential work. Every computer designed in the past three decades is built around hiding that gap. The answer the industry converged on is the **memory hierarchy**: a stack of five layers, each faster and smaller than the one below it, each trading off speed against capacity and cost per byte.

This lecture walks through that stack from the top (registers, living inside the CPU core next to the ALU) down to the bottom (magnetic hard drives, with physical platters and head arms). Along the way we introduce the two phenomena that make caching work at all — temporal and spatial locality — and the single most important number every programmer should internalize: the cost of a cache miss, measured not in nanoseconds but in the *instructions you could have run instead*.

The lecture is purely conceptual; hands-on inspection of the hierarchy on real hardware belongs to Lab 1.5 (`lscpu`, `free -h`, `lsblk`). Here we build the mental model that makes those commands meaningful.

## 1. Registers — the fastest layer

### Background

The ALU built up in Lecture 1.2 can only add numbers sitting right next to it. Lecture 1.3 introduced registers as the CPU's on-die storage slots that hold those numbers. This lecture re-anchors that concept before extending outward: computation happens on registers, period. Every instruction a CPU executes is "load from a register, compute, write to a register" — even when the program looks like it's manipulating RAM.

### How it works

A register is a cluster of flip-flops (typically 64 of them for a 64-bit register) hard-wired into the datapath next to the ALU. Access time is effectively zero — one clock cycle, ~0.3 ns on a 3.5 GHz chip. x86-64 exposes 16 general-purpose registers; ARM AArch64 exposes 31. Total register state in a modern core is roughly 1 KB. That KB is all the data the CPU can operate on *directly*; anything else has to be loaded first.

## 2. The memory wall

### Background

If registers are so fast, why not make everything a register? Two reasons: registers have to sit physically next to the ALU (limited silicon area), and the circuits that make them fast also make them expensive per byte. Meanwhile, CPUs have gotten roughly 100× faster over the last two decades while DRAM latency has barely moved. That widening gap — called the **memory wall** — is why modern programs spend most of their execution time waiting for data, not computing on it.

### How it works

A single trip to DRAM costs ~100 ns ≈ 350 cycles ≈ up to ~1,400 instructions the CPU could have issued. The CPU literally stalls. Every layer in the hierarchy exists to turn "1,000 cycles wasted" into "a handful of cycles wasted" for the common case.

## 3. L1 / L2 / L3 Cache

### Background

Caches exist because of a single empirical observation: programs reuse recently-touched data and data near recently-touched data. If a small copy of that data lives physically close to the CPU, most memory operations can avoid the 100 ns DRAM trip. One cache isn't enough — a small cache is fast but misses often; a big cache catches everything but becomes slow. The industry's answer: *multiple* caches, each at a different compromise point.

### How it works

- **L1**: ~32 KiB, ~4 cycles, per-core, split into L1-data and L1-instruction. Built from SRAM (6 transistors per bit; no capacitor, no refresh).
- **L2**: ~256 KiB – 1 MiB, ~10–12 cycles, per-core.
- **L3**: ~2 – 32 MiB, ~30–40 cycles, shared across cores.
- **DRAM**: 1 transistor + 1 capacitor per bit; must refresh every ~64 ms; ~100 ns access.

The transistor-count difference between SRAM (6T) and DRAM (1T + 1C) is why SRAM is faster *and* more expensive per byte (6× the silicon). Capacitors leak; SRAM's cross-coupled inverters don't. That single physical asymmetry is why caches and main memory are built from different circuits entirely.

## 4. Locality of reference

### Background

Caching is a gamble. The hardware guesses that you'll touch address X again soon (**temporal locality**) and that you'll also touch its neighbors X+1, X+2 (**spatial locality**), and it pre-loads accordingly. The gamble works because real programs exhibit both patterns almost universally: loop counters and hot variables drive temporal locality; array traversal and instruction streams drive spatial locality.

### How it works

Caches fetch data in fixed-size chunks called **cache lines** — typically 64 bytes (eight consecutive 8-byte words). When you read one `int` from an array, the CPU quietly hauls in the next 15 for free. If your next access is a neighbor, it's a cache hit at zero cost. If you jump randomly through memory, each access triggers a fresh 64-byte fetch and most of those bytes go unused — the worst case. Iterating a million-element array sequentially can be 10× faster than iterating it in random order, even though both do the same arithmetic.

## 5. RAM vs SSD vs HDD

### Background

Everything in the hierarchy above this line is volatile — it vanishes when you cut power. Registers, caches, RAM: all gone. Anything you want to keep across a reboot (files, the operating system itself, your photos) has to live in persistent storage. The price of persistence is another 1,000× to 100,000× slowdown compared to RAM.

### How it works

- **RAM (DRAM)**: ~100 ns latency, ~20–50 GB/s bandwidth, volatile, ~$3/GB.
- **NVMe SSD**: NAND flash cells that trap electrons in a floating gate. No moving parts. ~50–150 μs random-read latency — 500 to 1,500× slower than RAM — but sequential bandwidth of 3–7 GB/s, which can rival RAM on *throughput*. ~$0.08/GB.
- **HDD**: Magnetized platters with a physical head arm that has to seek (~9 ms) and wait for the platter to rotate (~4 ms at 7,200 RPM). ~10 ms random-access latency — *100,000× slower than RAM*. ~$0.02/GB.

The biggest misconception to defuse: "SSDs are basically RAM now." They're not. SSD *bandwidth* approaches RAM, but SSD *latency* remains 1,000× higher. Bandwidth is "how much per second"; latency is "how long before the first byte arrives." The two numbers behave very differently, and latency is what dominates for random-access workloads.

## 6. Cache misses matter more than you think

### Background

A single L3 miss costs 100–300 cycles. A modern core can issue 4 instructions per cycle. That's up to 1,000 instructions of potential work the CPU could have done while it waited for one value from DRAM. Beginner performance intuition focuses on "how many operations does my algorithm do" — but on modern hardware, *how your data is laid out* often matters more than *what computation you're doing*.

### How it works

Total CPU time = execute cycles + stall cycles. If the working set fits in L1, stalls are near zero. If it spills to L2, ~10 cycles per miss. L3, ~40. DRAM, ~350. An algorithm that is O(n log n) on paper can be slower than an O(n²) one in practice if the O(n²) touches memory sequentially and the O(n log n) jumps around. This is why "array of structs vs. struct of arrays" layout decisions can deliver 10× speedups without changing a line of logic.

### Visual treatment

Animation #9 (`the-stall`) brings the lecture home: a `CPU` ticking through instructions at a steady beat; one instruction needs a value that's not in cache; the instruction-counter freezes; a "waiting for DRAM" bar crawls for ~350 cycles while a counter shows "instructions you could have run: 850 ... 900 ... 1,000+". The viewer leaves with the visceral understanding that one cache miss is a lot of silence.

## Lecture Design Decisions

### Narrative arc: start with a puzzle, not a definition

We open with a question the viewer can feel physically: "Your laptop has 16 GB of RAM. RAM is fast. So why does your CPU have *three* levels of cache?" That question motivates the entire pyramid — every layer of the hierarchy exists as an answer to it. Concept 2 (the memory wall) is what gives us the right to ask it.

### Pyramid as the spine

Animation #2 (the `HierarchyPyramid`) is the central visual. Every subsequent animation is implicitly situated on one tier of this pyramid, and the narration gestures back at it by name. If the viewer remembers nothing else, they should remember the shape of that pyramid and the direction of its two axes.

### No CLI, no code

This is conceptual territory. Commands like `lscpu` and `free -h` belong to Lab 1.5. A viewer who has not yet done Lab 1.5 should still follow this lecture completely; a viewer who has done Lab 1.5 should recognize the tiers they saw in the tool output.

### Out of scope

Cache associativity, replacement policies (LRU), write-back vs. write-through, the TLB, NUMA effects, cache coherence protocols (MESI), persistent memory (Optane), CXL memory, and SIMD-driven cache behavior. All of those are later-section material. The goal here is to plant the hierarchy and the concept of cache misses firmly enough that every future lecture can say "and then a cache miss" and the viewer nods.
